What was the process of using AI like?

Using AI in a formal research based context was interesting as the only experience we had with it was casual conversationalist interactions. Often it is used to assist people with skills theyre struggling with and offer advice but it is also used to analyse large amounts of data. The latter is how we used AI in this project. First, we used AI to analyse all the comments we had together and as their individual catergories. From there one engine (Gemini) gave us an indepth analysis of what were the core themes across the postive and negative of each catergory, while another engine (Copilot) provided us with statistics for the data that we could then put into a chart. Next, we researched how to form an effective prompt for creating images and each stuck to similar phrasing that would ensure we recieved similar image outputs from the engines. We asked each of the AIs to create an image for the positive, negative and combined comments for each catergory as well as the same for all the comments combined. Natasha then went further asking Gemini to create images based on individual comments.

How does AI add to the final project?

As we were looking at the local community's perception of mayflower street, we realised that some opinions contradicted others and could be exaggerated in moments of passion. Relying on them individually to form a reliable idea of the public perception of plymouth quickly became a challenging task with the growing amounts of data. We also connected the perception of AI to be controversial, similarly to what we were discovering in the social media research. By using AI we reinforce the idea that opinions of the public are often unreliable individually but when combinded with other knowledge can be a great resource to develop ideas. This also allowed us to consciously think critically about the outputs we recieved from both the AI engines and the social media research

What skills did you learn whilst using AI?

We learnt to think critically about AI outputs rather than using the engines passively. When developing prompts for visualising comments, we discovered that precise wording was essential for generating accurate, design‑relevant images. We also realised we couldn’t input all the comments and the prompt together; entering the comments first caused the AI to produce its own analysis without direction. Separating the steps taught us how to control the engine’s interpretation and guide it toward more intentional results.
